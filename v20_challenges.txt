CHALLENGE 2: SHIP HULL-DOWN (HULL DISAPPEARING FIRST)
======================================================================

  Observer eye height: 1.7 m
  Ship hull height: 10 m
  Ship mast height: 30 m

  GLOBE MODEL:
  Geometric horizon from 1.7m:          4.7 km
  Hull (10m) disappears at:             15.9 km
  Mast (30m) disappears at:             24.2 km
  Hull disappears FIRST, then mast → bottom-up disappearance ✅

  DOME/FLAT MODEL:
  On a flat surface, no geometric horizon exists.
  Objects shrink to a vanishing point via perspective.
  Hull angular size = atan(hull_height / distance)
  Hull (10m) drops below 1' resolution at:  34.4 km
  Mast (30m) drops below 1' resolution at:  103.1 km
  BOTH shrink proportionally → whole ship vanishes at once
  Perspective CANNOT explain bottom-up disappearance ❌

  OBSERVED:
  Ships at 10-20 km consistently disappear hull-first, bottom-up.
  Telescopes / zoom cameras can partially restore the hull at ~15 km
  but NOT at >20 km — indicating true geometric obstruction.

  DOME DEFENSE — ATMOSPHERIC LENSING:
  Dense air near water surface bends light upward, creating a
  refraction 'horizon' even on a flat surface. This CAN produce
  bottom-up disappearance with specific temperature gradients.
  However: this predicts the effect varies dramatically with
  weather, while observations show it's remarkably consistent.

  VERDICT: Globe model explains hull-down naturally and consistently.
  Dome model requires atmospheric lensing mechanism — possible but
  less parsimonious. ⚠️  ADVANTAGE: GLOBE

CHALLENGE 3: BEDFORD LEVEL EXPERIMENT
======================================================================

  Bedford Level canal: 9.7 km straight

  GLOBE — No refraction:
    Curvature drop = d²/(2R) = 9700²/(2×6371000)
    Drop = 7.38 m (738.4 cm)

  GLOBE — Standard refraction (k=0.13):
    Effective R = R/(1-k) = 7322989 m
    Apparent drop = 6.42 m (642.4 cm)

  DOME/FLAT — No refraction:
    Expected drop: 0.00 m (flat surface)

  REFRACTION ANALYSIS:
    k needed to reduce globe drop to <0.5m: k = 0.932
    Standard k = 0.13
    k values observed over water: 0.05 to 0.25 (normal)
    k values in extreme conditions: can reach 0.5+ (strong mirage)

  ROWBOTHAM (1838): Saw boat at full height → concluded flat
  WALLACE (1870): Saw boat drop below line → concluded curved
  MODERN: Both are correct for their atmospheric conditions.
    Rowbotham likely had k ≈ 0.3-0.5 (warm day over cool water)
    Wallace likely had k ≈ 0.1-0.15 (standard conditions)

  VERDICT: Bedford Level is INCONCLUSIVE as a test between models.
  Atmospheric refraction dominates at these distances. ⚖️  TIE

======================================================================

  Star trail analysis for 1-hour exposure:

  Location                     Lat Trail Pattern                  Dome Explanation
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  North Pole                   +90° CCW circles around Polaris     Observer directly under dome center.
                                                                  All dome objects rotate CCW around observer.
  Mid-North (45°N)             +45° CCW arcs around Polaris        Observer north of center. Looking up,
                                                                  dome rotates CCW. Polaris elevated 45°.
  Equator (0°)                  +0° Straight vertical trails E→W   Observer on dome edge. Dome rotates
                                                                  overhead → stars appear to rise/set vertically.
  Mid-South (45°S)             -45° CW arcs around σ Octantis      Observer beyond dome center (south side).
                                                                  Looking inward at dome → motion appears
                                                                  REVERSED (CW) from outside looking in.
  South Pole (-90°)            -90° CW circles around SCP          Observer at maximum dome distance.
                                                                  All dome rotation appears CW (reversed
                                                                  viewpoint, like reading clock from behind).

  OBSERVED in actual star trail photography:
    ✅ North Pole: CCW around Polaris — CONFIRMED
    ✅ Mid-north: CCW arcs around Polaris — CONFIRMED
    ✅ Equator: Near-vertical trails — CONFIRMED
    ✅ Mid-south: CW arcs around σ Octantis — CONFIRMED
    ✅ South Pole: CW circles around SCP — CONFIRMED

  THE KEY QUESTION: Why does the dome appear to rotate CW in the south?
  Dome model answer: The observer's viewpoint orientation flips when
  looking 'inward' from the outer dome rim. Like viewing a clock
  from behind the face — the hands appear to move counterclockwise.
  This is the same viewpoint flip we already use for azimuths.

  🌍 GLOBE: Earth rotates → stars appear to circle the celestial poles.
     Direction depends on which pole you face.
  🗺️  DOME: Dome rotates → stars circle. Southern observers 'face
     inward' → apparent reversal of rotation direction.
  VERDICT: Both models explain star trails. ⚖️  TIE
  HOWEVER: The dome model's 'viewpoint flip' is mathematically
  equivalent to having a second rotation center (south celestial pole),
  which is what the globe model describes. Same math, different label.
